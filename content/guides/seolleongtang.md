## What Is Seolleongtang?

**Seolleongtang (설렁탕)** is a milky-white beef bone soup that's one of Korea's most beloved traditional dishes. While purists might argue it's technically *tang* (soup) rather than *gukbap* (soup rice), it occupies the same cultural space and is almost always served with rice — making it a close cousin to gukbap.

The defining feature of seolleongtang is its **lactescent (milky white) broth**, achieved by simmering beef leg bones, knuckles, and oxtail for **10 to 15 hours** until the collagen and marrow dissolve into the water, creating a rich, creamy texture without adding any dairy.

## The History

Seolleongtang has remarkably deep roots. The name is believed to come from **Seonnongdan (선농단)**, an altar where sacrificial rituals were held during the Joseon Dynasty. The soup was traditionally served to workers and laborers because of its high nutritional value and ability to restore energy.

The most famous restaurant, **Imun Seolnongtang** in Jongno, Seoul, has been operating since **1904** — making it one of the oldest continuously operating restaurants in Korea.

## How It's Made

The process is deceptively simple but demands patience:

1. **Soak** — Beef bones are soaked in cold water for 1-2 hours to remove blood
2. **Blanch** — Bones are boiled briefly to remove impurities, then rinsed
3. **Simmer** — Bones are simmered on high heat for 10-15 hours, with water topped up as needed
4. **Strain** — The broth is strained to achieve a silky texture
5. **Season** — The broth is lightly seasoned with salt at the table (not during cooking)

## How to Eat Seolleongtang

1. **Taste the broth first** — Unseasoned, to appreciate its depth
2. **Add salt and pepper** — To your preference (the soup is served unseasoned)
3. **Add scallions** — Generous amounts of chopped scallions
4. **Add rice** — Either mix it in or eat on the side
5. **Add kimchi** — Many people add kimchi juice to the broth for extra depth
6. **Dip the meat** — The attached beef is often dipped in **saeujeot** (salted shrimp) and ssamjang

## The Perilla Connection

Like sundae gukbap, seolleongtang is often enjoyed with **perilla seed powder (deulkkae-garu)** and **perilla oil (deulkkae-gireum)**. A drizzle of perilla oil just before eating adds an incredible nutty aroma that elevates the entire experience.

Seolleongtang is traditionally recommended for:
- Post-illness recovery
- Lactating mothers (believed to boost milk production)
- Hangover recovery
- Cold weather warming

## Verdict

Seolleongtang is Korean soul food at its finest. The hours of simmering transform simple beef bones into something almost magical — a creamy, savory broth that's deeply nourishing. If you only try one Korean soup, this should be a top contender.
